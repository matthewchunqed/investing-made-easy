Implementing Those Ideas Into Your Portfolio

The ideal portfolio should contain only stocks which are discounted by market risk. And it should be diversified as well as possible, to reduce diversifiable risk (which lowers median expected returns) and to ensure it holds all the mega-winners. But all stocks are exposed to market risk, because market risk is the risk you take for investing in the market at all[6].

Thus, a portfolio of all the stocks in the world will be the best-diversified portfolio to capture market risk – and VT (the ticker symbol for the Vanguard Total World Stock Fund) is that portfolio. VT bundles all the world’s stocks together into a single convenient low-cost fund. Worldwide diversification in particular is important for any rational portfolio – Japan was once the world’s second largest and fastest growing economy, and it suffered a crash 30 years ago that it still hasn’t recovered from. Even though a single country may never recover from a crash, the world as a whole will.

As long as your investments are diversified, the most threatening risk to your profits is your own psychological tolerance to short-term volatility. Investors often panic-sell during crashes and this can cripple long-term goals. If you want to reduce psychological risk, and suspect you might panic if the market crashes, you can reduce volatility by adding BNDW, an index of all the world’s bonds. Use a resource like this to help you decide if you’d like to reduce psychological risk, and by how much.

When a company decides to lower its stock value and give you that lowered value as cash, that is called a dividend. Ensure you automatically reinvest dividends for all purchased funds, or else they will become uninvested over time. And expense ratios compound, so they must be kept as low as possible. A 1% expense ratio means after 40 years you will only have

(99%)40 = 0.9940 = 67% of your money, meaning the expense cost you a third of your retirement funds, while a 0.06% expense ratio will leave you with 0.999440 = 97.6% after 40 years.

As long as you have taxable income during a year, you can contribute that income to a Roth or Trad IRA and a Roth or Trad 401K for retirement and get a tax advantage. Roth contributions are taxed as income the year you make them, whereas Trad contributions are taxed as income during retirement, so contribute Roth whenever your current income is less than your expected income during retirement and you should contribute Trad when your current income is more than your retirement income. Withdrawing money from your Trad IRA/401K and standard investment accounts does count as income, while Roth withdrawals do not count. If your tax bracket is the same during retirement and employment, it does not matter which one you choose because of the commutativity of multiplication. That is,

(20% lost from tax) 0.8 * 1.08 * 1.08 * … * 1.08 (market gains) = 1.08 * 1.08 * 1.08 * … * 0.8


For most people, investing has a “best strategy” Buy VT and contribute to a Roth/Trad IRA & 401K. Do not sell until retirement, especially in response to underperformance. Find an appropriate psychological risk vs. return tradeoff, choose to contribute Roth vs. Trad based on your current tax bracket, and ensure you reinvest dividends automatically.
