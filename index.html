Investing Made Easy

A Short Guide to Evidence-Based Investing

The Key Ideas

When you buy a company’s stock, you buy a fraction of that company. The price of that company is dictated entirely by its potential buyers, and supply-and-demand. Things such as earnings reports & new announcements may increase or decrease the prices we (the buyers) demand. We profit off the difference in price between now and when we want to sell, so we can maximize investments by buying assets whose price we can expect to increase.

Every year, the price of stocks in the market as a whole grows an average of 7% (ignoring inflation). But if we are the ones who set the prices – doesn’t that mean we’re pricing this year’s market 7% too low? And if we can expect increases, on average, every year then isn’t investing just free money?

The answer is no, investing is not free money – because you are always taking a risk.

Definition Risk is the ability for your money to not be there when you need it to be there.

Money invested in the market grows 7% on average, but with high spread – some years will return negative %, and some years will skyrocket in growth. By investing in any company, you may lose all your money if certain catastrophic events happen: wars, natural disasters, or supply chain disruptions are all risks that affect all companies. These risks are undiversifiable risks, because there is no way to make a portfolio containing stocks that isn’t exposed to these risks.

Nobody would subject their money to these risks without expecting additional profits as a reward for taking that risk. For the same reason that banks won’t risk lending their money to you without requiring you to pay interest, investors can demand a 7% discount on next year’s expected stock prices as payment for taking on market risk. This risk-driven discount, named “the market premium”, is why investors can expect a profit (on average) each year. By exposing yourself to market risk in a sensible way, you can expect profits while doing zero speculation.

Not all risk has a positive expected return. Some risks can be minimized with diversification. The impact of company-specific risks (such as a PR scandal), or sector-specific risk (such as sector regulations), or even country-specific risk (such as trade wars) can be minimized by buying stocks from every company in the entire world.

Even though stocks grow 7% on average each year, this is highly skewed by a small group of mega-winners with most stocks underperforming. Each stock, over its lifetime, has only a 31% chance of outperforming the market, meaning stock picking is like flipping a very unfair coin. And only the top 4% of stocks result in all the net wealth creation in the market – meaning a portfolio of the bottom 96% of all stocks would return the expected risk-free return rate.[5] That means that diversification both reduces risk and increases your expected returns, because a portfolio containing all stocks is guaranteed to contain the 31% which outperform the market, and would contain the 4% of mega-winner stocks that result in the expected annual market gains, before the gain has occurred.

For that reason, most returns come from taking undiversifiable risk, not “choosing the best companies”. Attempting to pick winning stocks typically results in underperformance or catastrophic losses, while attempting to pick the best fund managers typically results in similar levels of risk while also underperforming due to paying compounding fees.

For example, Warren Buffet (famous for his savvy investments) has statistically insignificant returns in excess of the risk he took[1], meaning his success could be entirely replicated and improved, by targeting the same undiversifiable risks he once targeted. The vast majority of professional portfolio managers underperform a risk-adjusted benchmark and managers who do well are unlikely to continue doing well[2], and even in the best instances, fund managers cannot deliver more profits than the cost of their fees (in a statistically significant way)[3][4]. So while most people think the stock market demands you speculate to “buy the winning stocks”, the data has shown that even professionals find this exceptionally hard to do consistently. And in a market where it is infeasible to predict what stocks will win, before anyone else does, the most rational way to profit is by getting paid to take undiversifiable risk via the market premium.

This matches what economic theory would predict. Imagine you knew Amazon stock was going to increase by $50 tomorrow. You can take a big loan and buy as much of it as possible. You could even buy a speculative financial product (like a long or a call) which will pay you if the stock increases. By tomorrow, you’ll profit without any additional risk since you knew the stock would increase while the prices reflected in the market didn’t.

But now, assume half the world could read the future as well as you can. Then, every person in the informed half rushes to buy Amazon stock, longs, calls, etc. increasing their demand until they cost $50 more – the “correct” price. And at the same time, the only people who would be willing to sell Amazon equity at the lower price would be the half of the world who is uninformed. That means the informed half of the world would immediately correct the price, by buying at the expense of the uninformed half.

This shows there are only two ways to profit in the stock market: being paid to take an undiversifiable risk, or bringing new information to the market that is not already reflected in prices. There are two key issues with the latter strategy. First, if you are uninformed or slow, the others will profit at your expense. Second, the ability to bring new information to the market disappears as soon as it’s known, whereas the ability to profit from risk discounts must always exist.

This is why diversification can both increase your expected returns and decrease your expected risk. If you expect the market will grow in value every year by 7% on average (due to the risk discount), but that growth is powered entirely by just 4% of its companies, the data shows it is far more profitable to buy the haystack rather than compete to find the needle.

The most important resource is time in the market. Over time, your bad years will even out against your good years and your actual results will approach your expected results. That means not investing is the most risky choice for meeting your long term financial goals, whereas stocks is the most risky choice for money you need today.

Test Your Knowledge: Economic Data

Imagine a new tariff is announced which will impact the economy. When will stock prices change?

(A): Stock prices will change immediately after the tariff is announced.

(B): Stock prices will change when the tariff takes effect, which is when jobs and companies start to be impacted.

(C): Stock prices will some months or years after the tariff takes effect, once investors have the economic data to know exactly how much tariffs affected their profits.

Solution

The answer is A. As soon as the tariff is announced, active investors will predict how much the tariffs will impact their future profits and immediately adjust how much they are willing to buy and sell stocks for.

Today's stock prices are estimates of future earnings, not of past information. So stocks can do well in bad economic times or do poorly in good economic times, because prices reflect future optimism rather than current real circumstances.

Stock changes can also correct during (C) if investors gain info at that time that their predictions made during (A) were inaccurate, but this will likely be just a small correction.

The important idea here is that stock prices only change when new information about future profits, relative to current expectations enter the market.

Test Your Knowledge: Portfolio Recommendations

All five investments in the following portfolio, and their reasoning, are at odds with modern investment knowledge. Do you know why?

40% in the S&P 500 – the 500 most profitable companies in the world. The best companies in the world are usually the best investments.

20% in a fund holding major tech companies. AI is going to be extremely profitable in the next few years so I should invest now before those companies make those profits.

20% in a fund holding all US companies. The US market has outperformed the rest of the world over long stretches of time, and because of that I know the US is a more profitable place to invest than all other countries.

10% in Tesla stock. Tesla is a very risky investment due to Elon Musk's questionable business acumen, and higher risk means higher rewards.

10% in a stock recommended as a "Strong Buy" by professionals on a highly respected finance website.

Hint

Prices are determined by investor demand, so the companies with the brightest futures have the highest prices.

Solutions

All five investments lower expected returns by lowering diversification, and the reasoning for each one's future performance is insufficient

For a stock to have high future profits, it must have a bright future which is not already reflected in prices. So to know a stock will outperform the market, you have to know something that most or all other investors do not know. In other words, you have to have evidence to believe in a company more than everyone else, and then you would have to be right.

S&P 500: The most profitable companies in the world also have the highest expectations already priced in. Unless they exceed those expectations even more (which can and has happened), they will not outperform market.

Tech Fund: Stocks give investors access to future profits, not current profits. Today's profits don't decide today's stock prices – only expected future profits do. A company's stock price rises once information about future expectations becomes known, not when those profits actually materialize.

US Fund: Recent outperformance means expectations for the US's future profits have recently increased. Unless you have evidence that the US will outperform already high expectations even more, recent outperformance gives no info on future growth.

Tesla: This is diversifiable risk, so its impact on an investor's portfolio can be minimized with diversification. Since buyers can construct a portfolio that owns Tesla while avoiding diversifiable risk, sellers do not need to offer a discount for it.

Strong Buy Stock: By the time you see the headline, high-frequency investors will have already bought the stock and increased the price, or the finance website would have done it before publishing. Unless you are the first to bring this info to the market, the prices have already increased.
