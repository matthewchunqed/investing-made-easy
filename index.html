Investing with Evidence

How academic research replaces speculation with rational strategy.

The Efficiency Principle

The core of rational investing is the **Efficient Market Hypothesis**. In a world of instant communication, information travels at the speed of light. By the time you read a news headline about a stock, the price has likely already adjusted to reflect that news.

For a beginner, this is actually good news. It means you don't have to be a "genius" to get a fair price; the market is doing the hard work of pricing for you.

The Core Insight If markets are efficient, the only way to get higher returns is to take on more systemic risk, not by trying to outsmart other traders.

Quantifying Risk

Academic research uses math to separate "luck" from "skill." One of the most famous formulas is the Sharpe Ratio, which helps us understand if a return is worth the "rollercoaster" ride required to get it:

$$S_p = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_p}$$

Essentially, we are looking for the most "bang for our buck." We want the highest possible return ($R_p$) for every unit of volatility ($\sigma_p$) we endure.

The Diversification Shield

Diversification is often called the "only free lunch" in finance. By spreading your investments across thousands of companies globally, you eliminate the risk of any single company going to zero affecting your livelihood.

[Image of a diversified portfolio pie chart]

When you own the whole market, you are betting on human ingenuity and global growth rather than the survival of a single CEO or product. It is the ultimate rational hedge against the unknown.
