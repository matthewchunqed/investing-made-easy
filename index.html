The Ideas

Every year, the price of stocks in the market as a whole grows an average of 7% (ignoring inflation). But if we are the ones who set the prices – doesn’t that mean we’re pricing this year’s market 7% too low? And if we can expect increases, on average, every year then isn’t investing just free money?

The answer is no, investing is not free money – because you are always taking a risk.

Definition: Risk Risk is the ability for your money to not be there when you need it to be there.

Money invested in the market grows 7% on average, but with high spread – some years will return negative %, and some years will skyrocket in growth. By investing in any company, you may lose all your money if certain catastrophic events happen: wars, natural disasters, or supply chain disruptions are all risks that affect all companies. These risks are undiversifiable risks, because there is no way to make a portfolio containing stocks that isn’t exposed to these risks.

Nobody would subject their money to these risks without expecting additional profits as a reward for taking that risk. For the same reason that banks won’t risk lending their money to you without requiring you to pay interest, investors (when deciding what price to buy at) can demand a 7% discount on next year’s expected stock prices as payment for taking on market risk. This risk-driven discount, named “the market premium”, is why investors can expect a profit (on average) each year. By exposing yourself to market risk in a sensible way, you can expect profits while doing zero speculation.

The Core Insight If markets are efficient, your goal isn't to find "undervalued" stocks, but to choose a level of risk that matches your long-term goals.

The Sharpe Ratio

How do we measure if a return was actually "earned" or just a result of blind luck? We use the Sharpe Ratio to calculate the excess return per unit of risk.

$$S_p = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_p}$$

A higher ratio indicates a more "efficient" investment—you are getting more return for the amount of stress (volatility) the portfolio causes.

Strategic Diversification

Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT) argues that you can reduce risk without necessarily reducing return. This is achieved by holding assets that don't move in perfect lockstep.

[Image of a diversified portfolio pie chart]

By holding thousands of global companies, the failure of one is offset by the success of another. This "diversification shield" is the only rational way for a beginner to survive the inherent uncertainty of global markets.
